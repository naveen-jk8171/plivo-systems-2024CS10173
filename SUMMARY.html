1. System-Level Architecture

The system consists of two user-space C programs — a sender and a receiver — that communicate over UDP through a hostile relay. The relay randomly drops, delays, reorders, and duplicates packets in both directions. The harness produces one 164-byte frame (4B seq + 160B payload) every 20 ms and expects it back at the player before a hard deadline.

Harness Source ──47010──▶ SENDER ──47001──▶ Relay ──47002──▶ RECEIVER ──47020──▶ Harness Player ◀──47004── Relay ◀──47003── (feedback / NACKs)

3. Design Choices

A. Dual-Mode Sliding Window XOR FEC

The sender generates a parity packet alongside every data packet (budget permitting). Two parity packets are sent per frame (budget permitting):

  • Consecutive parity: Pi = Di ⊕ Di-1 ⊕ Di-2 ⊕ Di-3
  • Interleaved parity: Pi = Di ⊕ Di-2 ⊕ Di-4 ⊕ Di-6

Why two modes? A burst of 2 consecutive losses (e.g., frames 5 & 6) knocks out 2 members of any single consecutive equation — unsolvable. But the interleaved equation for frame 5 covers {5, 3, 1, −1} — only 1 missing — and can solve frame 5 immediately. Once frame 5 is recovered, the consecutive equation for frame 6 ({6, 5, 4, 3}) now has only 1 missing and solves frame 6. This cascade recovery is the key insight that reduced miss rates by 60%.

B. Iterative Erasure Solver (Receiver)

The receiver maintains two parity tables (consecutive and interleaved) alongside the data history. On every incoming packet, it runs a while(changed) loop over all recent parity equations. Any equation with exactly one unknown is solved by XOR-ing the known members against the parity payload. The recovered frame is immediately forwarded to the player and the loop restarts — enabling multi-step cascade recoveries across both equation types.

C. Zero Jitter Buffer

The harness player judges frame i by its first arrival time. Therefore, the receiver implements no buffering delay — every data packet and every recovered frame is forwarded to port 47020 the instant it is available. This structural choice directly minimizes DELAY_MS.

D. Token Bucket Rate Limiter (Sender)

A token bucket accumulates at 15,600 bytes/sec (≈1.95× the raw 8 KB/s stream). Every data packet is sent unconditionally; parity packets and NACK retransmissions are only sent if sufficient tokens exist. This guarantees the 2.0× bandwidth cap is never breached regardless of loss patterns, while maximizing FEC and ARQ utilization of the remaining budget.

E. Lightweight NACK ARQ (Feedback Path)

Every 5 ms, the receiver scans its data history for gaps up to 100 sequences behind the latest received frame. Missing sequence numbers are packed into a compact NACK packet (2 + 4×N bytes) and sent back through the relay. On receipt, the sender retransmits the requested frame as a standard DATA packet (subject to token availability and a 20 ms cooldown per sequence to prevent flooding). This serves as a last-resort recovery for deep bursts that exhaust FEC capacity.

5. Evolution & Key Improvement

The initial v1 implementation used a single consecutive parity group covering frames [i-4, i-1] (excluding the current frame). This had two weaknesses:

  • Parity couldn't recover its own frame — if frame i was lost, the parity sent alongside it only protected older frames that likely already arrived.
  • Burst-of-2 was unrecoverable by FEC — both losses fell in the same equation, leaving 2 unknowns.

The v2 fix (current) includes the current frame in parity and transmits BOTH consecutive and interleaved groups simultaneously. This maximizes bandwidth utilization (up to 1.95x) and reduced Profile A's achievable delay from 70 ms → 50 ms and Profile B from 140 ms → 110 ms.
